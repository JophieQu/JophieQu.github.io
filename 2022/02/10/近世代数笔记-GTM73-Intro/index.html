Let f:A→Bf:A\rightarrow Bf:A→B and g:B→Cg:B\rightarrow Cg:B→C be two mappings.

fff and ggg injective   ⟹  \implies⟹ gfgfgf is injective; (9)
fff and ggg surjective   ⟹  \implies⟹ gfgfgf is surjective; (10)
gfgfgf injective   ⟹  \implies⟹ fff is injective; (11)
gfgfgf surjective   ⟹  \implies⟹ ggg​ is surjective. (12)

Solution.

(9) For all aaa, a′∈Aa'\in Aa′∈A, a≠a′  ⟹  f(a)≠f(a′)a≠a'\implies f(a)≠f(a')a​=a′⟹f(a)​=f(a′)

Let b=f(a)b=f(a)b=f(a), b′=f(a′)b'=f(a')b′=f(a′), and it follows from the definition of injectivity that g(b)≠g(b′)g(b)≠g(b')g(b)​=g(b′), i.e. $g(f(a))≠g(f(a’)) $ . □\square□

(10) is similar to (9), omitted.

(11) Suppose that fff​ is not injective. Then there exist aaa​, a′∈Aa'\in Aa′∈A​ with a≠a′a≠a'a​=a′​ and f(a)=f(a′)f(a)=f(a')f(a)=f(a′)​. So we have g(f(a))=g(f(a′))g(f(a))=g(f(a'))g(f(a))=g(f(a′))​, which contradicts the definition. Therefore, fff​ is injective. □\square□​​​​

(12) is similar to (10), omitted.
